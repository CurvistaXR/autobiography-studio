# Adaptive Interview, Style, and Outline

Read this file before asking preference or interview questions.

## Preference card

Record:

- audience: self, family, friends, colleagues, or public readers
- length: concise, standard, or full-length
- voice: first person by default; third person only on request
- style template
- privacy posture: private, family-safe, or public-ready
- cover route: authorized photo, symbolic generated image, or typographic

Length guides are planning ranges, not promises: concise 15,000–30,000 Chinese characters, standard 40,000–80,000, full-length 90,000+. Draft chapter by chapter so a long book is not compressed into one response.

## Style templates

### `understated-memoir` — 朴实克制

Clear chronology, concrete detail, restrained emotion, short reflection, minimal rhetorical flourish. Best default for uncertain or sensitive histories.

### `warm-family` — 温暖家庭叙事

Relationships, homes, meals, sayings, rituals, intergenerational change, and gratitude. Protect minors and third-party privacy.

### `literary-nonfiction` — 文学非虚构

Scene-led openings, sensory detail, recurring motifs, time shifts with clear anchors, and reflective endings. Never let atmosphere invent facts.

### `entrepreneurial-journey` — 事业与创业历程

Decisions, constraints, teams, failures, pivots, systems, and lessons. Avoid promotional claims, inflated titles, and unsupported performance numbers.

### `oral-history` — 口述史

Preserve the user's voice, speech rhythm, attributed recollection, historical context, and transparent uncertainty. Use edited quotations only with confirmation.

If a user asks for a named living author's style, do not imitate it closely. Offer the nearest template and describe controllable traits such as pacing, warmth, scene density, sentence length, or reflective depth.

## Gap-based interview loop

1. Rank gaps by narrative importance, evidence weakness, and dependency on later chapters.
2. Choose one coherent theme per round.
3. Ask three to seven focused questions, fewer when the subject is sensitive.
4. Ask for concrete time, place, people, action, consequence, feeling, and later interpretation when relevant.
5. Summarize what was learned and what remains uncertain.
6. Let the user correct the summary.
7. Update timeline, people, facts, interview notes, and privacy decisions.
8. Select the next highest-value gap.

Do not ask for facts already present in the materials unless resolving a conflict or inviting reflection.

## Coverage map

Use the map as a gap detector, not a fixed questionnaire:

- ancestry, birthplace, childhood home, caregivers, siblings, early memories
- school, teachers, friends, skills, formative books or events
- first work, mentors, craft, career changes, leadership, entrepreneurship
- love, partnership, parenting, friendship, community, loss
- hardship, health, migration, failure, conflict, recovery
- turning points, achievements, regret, luck, sacrifice
- values, faith, habits, money beliefs, identity, social change
- present life, legacy, unfinished work, message to future readers

## Outline gate

After gaps are sufficiently covered, offer:

- three to five title/subtitle pairs
- one recommended chronological or thematic structure
- chapter title, time span, central question, key scenes, evidence status, and estimated length
- front matter, back matter, chronology, photo placements, and source note

Show the complete outline in one reviewable artifact. Revise until user confirmation, then record the approved title, version, and timestamp in `project-status.json`.
